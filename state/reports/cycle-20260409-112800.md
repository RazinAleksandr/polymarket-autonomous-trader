### Post-Mortem: Iran Ceasefire Loss

**What happened:** On April 7-8, Pakistan brokered a surprise two-week ceasefire between the US and Iran. Trump agreed to halt military operations; Iran committed to reopening the Strait of Hormuz. The ceasefire was announced just 2 days after our entry.

**Root Cause Analysis:**

**Primary: Information Gap.** We missed Pakistan's active mediation role. Our analysis focused on Turkey, Egypt, and Qatar — all stalled. Pakistan's PM Shehbaz Sharif had been engaged behind the scenes, but we didn't search for "Pakistan Iran mediation" despite Pakistan being Iran's neighbor with historical mediation relationships.

**Secondary: Reasoning Error — Over-Applied Status Quo Bias.** We cited Rule 11 ("status quo wins ~70% of the time") and concluded the war would continue. But we failed to recognize that the Hormuz ultimatum (deadline April 6, THE DAY AFTER our entry) fundamentally changed the game. Deadlines with severe consequences create negotiation pressure that can override status quo inertia. We treated the ultimatum as an "escalation signal" when it was actually a "negotiation catalyst."

**Contributing: Violated Politics Rule 3.** Our own knowledge base (politics.md Rule 3) says "Never go above 90% or below 5% on geopolitical markets." We estimated 5% ceasefire = 95% NO, clearly violating this rule. If we had capped at 90% NO (10% ceasefire), the position would still have lost but the edge calculation might have changed our sizing or decision.

**Contributing: Timing Error.** We entered 1 day before the Hormuz ultimatum deadline. The deadline was the most significant catalyst for negotiation. Entering a high-conviction NO position on the eve of a major deadline was poor timing judgment.

**Contributing: Poor Risk-Reward.** At NO $0.90, the risk-reward was 0.11:1 ($33 potential profit vs $300 at risk). Even with high conviction, this ratio made the trade fragile — a small probability event wipes out the position entirely.

**Lessons Extracted:**

```
LESSON 1: DEADLINES OVERRIDE STATUS QUO BIAS
When a powerful actor sets a hard deadline with severe consequences (Hormuz closure, 
military ultimatum), the status quo probability drops dramatically. Deadlines create 
negotiation pressure that changes the base rate from ~30% change to ~50%+ change.
Evidence: 1569627 "US-Iran ceasefire by Apr 15" — entered Apr 5 at NO $0.90, 
estimated 5% ceasefire. Hormuz deadline Apr 6. Pakistan brokered ceasefire Apr 7. 
P&L: -$300.77.
Action: When a credible deadline exists within 7 days of resolution, increase base 
probability of change by 2-3x. Re-evaluate Rule 11 applicability.
Applies to: All geopolitical markets

LESSON 2: RESPECT YOUR OWN CAP RULES
Our politics.md Rule 3 says cap geopolitical probabilities at 90% / floor at 5%. We 
estimated 95% NO, violating this rule. The cap exists for a reason — geopolitical 
events are inherently unpredictable.
Evidence: Same trade. 95% NO estimate, actual YES. If capped at 90%, edge would have 
been 0.5pp, likely a SKIP.
Action: Hard-enforce the 90/10 cap on all geopolitical probability estimates.
Applies to: All politics/geopolitics

LESSON 3: HIGH-PRICE NO TRADES HAVE TERRIBLE RISK-REWARD
At NO $0.90+, you risk $300 to gain $33 (0.11:1). Even if you're right 95% of the 
time, the 5% loss wipes out 9 wins. This is structurally fragile.
Evidence: Same trade. $300 risk, $33 max profit. Single loss erased all prior 
unrealized gains.
Action: Apply a risk-reward floor of 0.20:1 for new positions. At NO $0.90, this 
means requiring even larger edges or smaller positions.
Applies to: All categories
```
